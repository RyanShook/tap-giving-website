8 NFC Giving Wins to Celebrate in Your First Month

You launched NFC giving. Now what? Here are 8 milestones to watch for, track, and celebrate with your team and congregation in the first 30 days.

April 12, 2026
7 min read

Win #1: Your First NFC Gift

Usually happens: Sunday 1

The very first person to tap and give. It might be a tech-savvy 20-something. It might be a curious deacon. Whoever it is, this is your proof-of-concept moment. Screenshot the transaction in your giving dashboard (redact the name) and text it to your leadership team. Momentum starts here.

How to track: Your giving platform (Tithely, Pushpay, etc.) will show digital gifts by date and source. Filter for your Sunday service window.

Win #2: A First-Time Visitor Gives

Usually happens: Weeks 1–2

When a name you don’t recognise shows up in your giving dashboard, that’s a visitor who gave. This is the most valuable NFC win. Without NFC, that person almost certainly would have left without giving. Now you have their contact info (from the giving receipt), and you can follow up with a personal thank-you.

Why it matters: A first-time gift is the #1 predictor of whether a visitor becomes a regular member and giver. More on this →

Win #3: Someone Says “That Was So Easy”

Usually happens: Week 1

This is the testimonial moment. When someone walks up to you after service and says, “I can’t believe how easy that was,” ask if you can share that next week. A quote from a real person in your congregation is 10x more persuasive than anything you can say from the stage.

How to use it: Share the quote (with permission) from the stage the following Sunday: “Last week, [name] told me, ‘I can’t believe how easy that was.’ If you haven’t tried it yet, give it a shot today.”

Win #4: Double-Digit Taps in a Single Service

Usually happens: Week 2

When you go from 5–8 taps in week one to 15–25 in week two, the snowball has started. Social proof is kicking in. People saw others tapping last week, and now they’re trying it. This is the week you should share the number from the stage (participation count, not dollar amounts).

Benchmark: A 100-person church typically sees 8–15 NFC gifts in week one, growing to 25–40 by week four.

Win #5: Your First Recurring Sign-Up Via NFC

Usually happens: Weeks 2–3

When someone taps, gives, and toggles “make this recurring,” you just gained a giver who will give every month for an average of 3+ years. That single tap could be worth $1,800+ over its lifetime ($50/month × 36 months). This is the win that makes your finance team smile.

Power move: Set your NFC URL to default to “recurring” mode. Many platforms support this via URL parameters. How to set this up →

Win #6: Cash Counting Takes Half the Time

Usually happens: Weeks 3–4

As more people shift to digital via NFC, the cash volume drops. Your counting team finishes faster. Fewer envelopes, fewer coins, fewer trips to the bank. This is a win your volunteers will notice before you do. When your counters say, “We were done in 20 minutes instead of 45,” that’s NFC working.

Hidden value: Less cash = less security risk, fewer counting errors, and happier volunteers who can spend that hour in ministry instead.

Win #7: Average Gift Amount Increases

Usually happens: Week 4

Pull your giving report at the end of month one and compare average gift size to the previous month. Digital givers give 2–3x more than cash givers on average. As NFC moves people from cash to digital, your average gift amount will climb—often from $20–30 to $45–60.

How to track: Compare “average gift size” in your giving dashboard for the month before NFC vs. the month after.

Win #8: You Stop Having to Mention It

Usually happens: Week 4+

The ultimate win: NFC giving becomes so normalised that you stop introducing it. People tap as naturally as opening a hymnal. The plates are part of the furniture, and giving happens without a prompt. This is the “set it and forget it” moment every pastor dreams about.

What to do now: Occasionally mention NFC for visitors, but for regular attenders, it’s baked in. Focus your offering moment on impact stories, not giving instructions. 5 ways to talk impact, not money →
